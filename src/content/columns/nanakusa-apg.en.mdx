# Japan's seven herbs of spring are a miniature of the eudicots — an APG tour in a single bowl of rice porridge

> *Seri, nazuna, gogyō, hakobera, hotokenoza, suzuna, suzushiro — these are the seven herbs.*

Japanese schoolchildren memorize this little rhyme. But **recognizing all seven in the wild is a different story** — even most adults can only manage two or three.

In this column I re-sort these seven plants — the ones you eat in *nanakusa-gayu* (seven-herb rice porridge) on January 7 — using the **APG IV classification**. What comes out is surprising: the herbal set that Heian-era Japanese people assembled turns out to be a **beautifully balanced sampling of the eudicot tree of life**.

|---|---|---|---|

![Seri (Oenanthe javanica) — one of the seven herbs, from the Apiaceae](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Oenanthe_javanica.jpg)

First thing to notice: **the seven species are distributed across four families**. A couple of families are repeated, which tells us this isn't just "seven random spring weeds" — whoever chose them was **deliberately drawing from different lineages**, even though they didn't know it.

---

## The punchline: the seven herbs cover all three major eudicot lineages

Let me push this a step further. Let's place those four families on an APG IV phylogenetic tree.

- **Brassicaceae** (nazuna, turnip, daikon) → **Rosids** (Brassicales)
- **Apiaceae** (seri) → **Asterids** (Apiales)
- **Asteraceae** (hahakogusa, koonitabirako) → **Asterids** (Asterales)
- **Caryophyllaceae** (kohakobe) → **Caryophyllales** — neither Rosids nor Asterids; an independent lineage

Eudicots have three big "continents": **Rosids, Asterids, and Caryophyllales**. And the seven herbs **pull at least one representative from each one**.

![The seven herbs span all three major eudicot lineages](/columns/nanakusa-apg/eudicot-tree.svg)

In other words —

> **One bowl of nanakusa porridge lets you taste all three major lineages of the eudicots.**

This is genuinely impressive. About 70% of living angiosperm species are eudicots, and the seven herbs sweep the whole thing in just seven species.

## Why are they so spread out? A showcase of chemical weapons

The Heian Japanese had no knowledge of phylogenetics or evolution. So how did they manage to pick such a phylogenetically balanced set?

The answer lies in the **defensive chemistry** that each plant lineage independently evolved. Plants can't run, so each family evolved its own "chemical weapons" to fend off insects and herbivores. The seven herbs happen to tile these weapon classes almost without overlap.

- **Brassicaceae** (nazuna, turnip, daikon): [glucosinolates](/en/families/brassicaceae). When you grate or chew them, an enzymatic reaction releases isothiocyanates — the sharp pungency of daikon radish, the bite of turnip. Originally an insecticidal defense; humans reframed it as "flavor".
- **Apiaceae** (seri): [essential oils](/en/families/apiaceae). The distinctive aroma when you cut the stem. Mostly terpenes, meant to repel grazing animals. Humans call it "seri fragrance" and consider it a delicacy.
- **Asteraceae** (hahakogusa, koonitabirako): [sesquiterpene lactones](/en/families/asteraceae). The source of that slightly bitter note. Most of the "bitterness" in Japanese spring herbs actually comes from the Asteraceae.
- **Caryophyllaceae** (kohakobe): [saponins](/en/families/caryophyllaceae). Surfactant-like bitter compounds.

![Chemical defenses of the seven herbs — each family's unique weapon](/columns/nanakusa-apg/chemical-weapons.svg)

In short, the seven herbs are **a sampler of plant chemical weaponry — one dish each of "sharp, fragrant, bitter, and astringent", drawn from separate lineages.**

And these are all pharmacologically active. Eaten together, they deliver digestive stimulation (Brassicaceae pungency), appetite stimulation (Apiaceae aromatics), bile-flow promotion (Asteraceae bitters), and anti-inflammatory action (Caryophyllaceae saponins) — all aimed at a stomach exhausted from New Year's feasting. A 1,400-year-old functional food, and it holds up shockingly well.

## The "hotokenoza problem": relatives separated by 100 million years

The most famous headache around the seven herbs is the **hotokenoza problem**.

Have you ever seen a plant called "hotokenoza" with **reddish-purple lipped flowers**? On Plantour it's registered as [Hotokenoza (*Lamium amplexicaule*)](/en/plants/hotokenoza) — but **that is NOT the "hotokenoza" of the seven herbs**.

| | Modern "hotokenoza" | Seven-herbs "hotokenoza" |
|---|---|---|
| Scientific name | *Lamium amplexicaule* | *Lapsanastrum apogonoides* |
| Japanese name | Hotokenoza | Koonitabirako |
| Family | Lamiaceae | Asteraceae |
| Order | Lamiales | Asterales |

Both are within the **Asterids**, yes. But Lamiales and Asterales are estimated to have **diverged roughly 100 million years ago**. So **they share a name, but phylogenetically the gap between them is wider than the gap between humans and kangaroos.**

The edible one is the real seven-herb version (koonitabirako). The modern "hotokenoza" contains Lamiaceae-specific alkaloids and is not eaten. Easy mix-up — be careful.

![Modern "hotokenoza" (Lamium amplexicaule) — note the reddish-purple lipped flowers. This is NOT the seven-herbs version.](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Lamium_amplexicaule1.jpg)

## Capitula vs. compound umbels — two answers to "make small flowers look big"

One more interesting thing: **the flower architecture of Apiaceae vs. Asteraceae.**

Look closely at a seri flower and you'll see tiny white florets fanning out like an umbrella — a **compound umbel**. Now look at a hahakogusa (gogyō) "yellow head": what looks like a single flower is actually **dozens of miniature florets crowded into a disk** — a **capitulum** (head inflorescence).

The strategy is the same — "instead of one big flower, glue a lot of small flowers together to give pollinators a *bouquet deal*". But the implementation is different. Compound umbels and capitula evolved **independently on separate branches of the Asterids** — a textbook case of convergent evolution. ![Capitulum (Asteraceae) vs. compound umbel (Apiaceae) — two independent answers to "make small flowers look big"](/columns/nanakusa-apg/capitulum-vs-umbel.svg)

You can literally put seri and gogyō side by side on your kitchen counter and taste two independent answers to the same design problem.

## The Heian people didn't know APG, but...

Let me state the main claim plainly:

> **The seven herbs aren't a coincidence. "Pick one plant per lineage so that their functions don't overlap" is an empirical rule people worked out 1,400 years ago — and it happens to produce a perfect miniature of the APG tree.**

They didn't know phylogenetics. But searching for "herbs that are good after the New Year feast" led them to a set that samples Rosids, Asterids, and Caryophyllales, each with its own chemistry. Accumulated hands-on knowledge, **reaching into the structure of nature without waiting for the formal theory.**

And conveniently, these seven herbs connect all of Plantour's main features.

## Final thoughts

The seven herbs in nanakusa porridge aren't just a seasonal custom — they're **a neatly packaged sampler of the main points of angiosperm evolutionary history.**

Rosids, Asterids, Caryophyllales. Pungent, fragrant, bitter, astringent. Lamiales and Asterales, separated by 100 million years. Capitula and compound umbels, independently invented. Once you realize all of this lives in a single bowl of porridge, next January 7 is going to look a little different.
